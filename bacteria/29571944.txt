IL-17-Mediated Immunity Controls Skin Infection and T Helper 1 Response during
Experimental Microsporum canis Dermatophytosis.

Comment in
    J Invest Dermatol. 2018 Aug;138(8):1691-1693.

Despite worldwide prevalence of superficial mycoses, the immune response in
dermatophytosis has scarcely been investigated. In this study, we developed a
model of superficial skin infection in C57BL/6 mice with Microsporum canis, a
highly prevalent human pathogen. This model mimics mild inflammatory human
dermatophytosis, characterized by neutrophil recruitment and fungal invasion
limited to the epidermis and exhibits the establishment of a specific T helper
type 17 immune response during infection. By using IL-17RA- or IL-17A/F-deficient
mice we showed that, in the absence of a functional IL-17 pathway, M. canis
extensively colonizes the epidermis and promotes an exaggerated skin inflammation
and a shift to an IFN-γ-mediated (T helper type 1) response. IL-17 signaling was 
not involved in neutrophil influx to skin or fungal invasion to deeper tissues.
Finally, this study shows that skin langerin-expressing cells contribute to the
antifungal T helper type 17 response in vivo. In conclusion, these data directly 
show a dual function of IL-17 cytokines in dermatophytosis by controlling
superficial infection and down-modulating a T helper type 1 antifungal response.
