objectives: determine prospectively causative pathogens central nervous
system (cns) infections patients admitted tertiary referral hospital in
hanoi, vietnam.
methods: may 2007 december 2008, cerebrospinal fluid (csf) samples from
352 adults suspected meningitis encephalitis underwent routine testing,
staining (gram, ziehl-nielsen, india ink), bacterial culture polymerase chain
reaction targeting neisseria meningitidis, streptococcus pneumoniae, s. suis,
haemophilus influenzae type b, herpes simplex virus (hsv), varicella zoster virus
(vzv), enterovirus, 16s ribosomal rna. blood cultures clinically
indicated radiology also performed. patients classified having
confirmed suspected bacterial (bm), tuberculous (tbm), cryptococcal (crm),
eosinophilic (eom) meningitis, aseptic encephalitis/meningitis (aem),
neurocysticercosis others.
results: 352 (male: 66%) patients recruited: median age 34 years (range
13-85). 95/352 (27.3%) diagnoses laboratory confirmed one cranial
radiology: bm (n = 62), tbm (n = 9), aem (n = 19), crm (n = 5), and
neurocysticercosis (n = 1, cranial radiology). s. suis predominated cause 
of bm [48/62 (77.4%)]; listeria monocytogenese (n = 1), s. pasteurianus (n = 1)
and n. meningitidis (n = 2) infrequent. aem viruses were: hsv (n = 12), vzv 
(n = 5) enterovirus (n = 2). 5 patients eom. 262/352 (74.4%) patients 
with full clinical data, 209 (79.8%) hospital referrals 186 (71%) had
been antimicrobials. 21 (8%) patients died: tbm (15.2%), aem (10%), bm
(2.8%).
conclusions: infections lacked microbiological confirmation. s. suis the
most common cause bm setting. improved diagnostics needed for
meningoencephalitic syndromes inform treatment prevention strategies.
