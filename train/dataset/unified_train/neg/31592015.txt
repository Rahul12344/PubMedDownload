background: based retrospective study performed institution, 38% of
inpatients living human immunodeficiency virus (hiv) found a
medication error involving anti-retroviral (arv) and/or opportunistic
infection (oi) prophylaxis medications.
objective: determine impact dedicated hiv-trained clinical pharmacist
on arv oi prophylaxis medication error rates institution.
methods: prospective quality improvement project conducted six month
period assess impact dedicated hiv-trained clinical pharmacist the
arv oi prophylaxis medication error rates. irb approval received.
results: 144 patients included analysis, experienced a
combined 76 medication errors. compared historical control study conducted 
our institution, percent patients experienced medication error
remained stable (38% vs. 39%, respectively) error rate per patient was
similar (1.44 vs. 1.36, p=ns). percent medication errors were
corrected prior discharge increased 24% 70% median time to
error correction decreased 42 hours 11.5 hours (p<0.0001).
conclusions: errors relating arv oi prophylaxis medications remain frequent
in inpatient people living hiv/aids. multiple interventions were
implemented, arv oi prophylaxis medication errors corrected faster and
with greater frequency prior discharge, however, similar rates errors for
patients existed. dedicated hiv clinicians adequate training and
credentialing necessary manage specialized disease state to
reduce overall number medication errors associated hiv/aids.
